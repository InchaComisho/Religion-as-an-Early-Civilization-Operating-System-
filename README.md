# Religion as an Early Civilization Operating System:

A Structural Interpretation of Myth, Dualism, and Human Conflict

Author: Master (InchaComisho / InchaComusho)
Published: 2025.12.21

Abstract

This document proposes that the world’s major religious and mythological narratives were not originally designed to create conflict, but to prevent it.
Rather than viewing religion as a source of division, this perspective interprets religious texts as early Operating Systems (OS) for human civilization—tools for teaching coexistence, harmony, and non‑dualistic ethics in a pre-scientific world.

The central argument is that human conflict arises not from religion itself, but from dualistic interpretation—a worldview that divides reality into opposing categories such as good/evil, saved/damned, or us/them.
This dualistic reading distorts the original intent of religious narratives and leads civilizations toward predictable collapse patterns described in myths such as Armageddon and Ragnarök.

1. Introduction

Modern discussions often frame religion as a source of conflict.
However, when examined structurally, religious narratives across cultures share a surprising degree of coherence.
This coherence suggests intentional design rather than coincidence.

This paper argues:

• Religion originally functioned as a civilizational OS
• Myths encode natural laws, social dynamics, and warnings about dualism
• Conflict emerges when dualistic cultures misinterpret these texts
• End‑times myths describe the structural failure mode of dualistic civilizations

This framework allows religious traditions, myths, and philosophical systems to be understood as parts of a unified structure.

2. Religion as a Pre‑Scientific Operating System

Before scientific frameworks existed, societies needed a way to encode:

• Ethical norms
• Social cohesion
• Ecological awareness
• Conflict avoidance
• Long‑term survival strategies

Religious narratives served this purpose.
They were not intended as literal history, but as symbolic instruction manuals for maintaining harmony within human communities and with nature.

In this sense, religion was humanity’s earliest attempt at:

• Systems design
• Social engineering
• Behavioral regulation
• Ecological ethics

This interpretation resolves many apparent contradictions in religious texts.

3. The Forbidden Fruit as the Birth of Dualism

The story of Adam and Eve is often read as a moral failure.
However, structurally, it describes the moment humans adopted dualistic cognition:

• Good vs. evil
• Right vs. wrong
• Self vs. other
• God vs. human

Once dualism appears, harmony becomes impossible.
Thus, “expulsion from Eden” is not a punishment—it is a natural consequence of adopting a worldview that divides reality.

This interpretation aligns with non‑dual philosophies found in:

• Buddhism
• Taoism
• Indigenous cosmologies
• Mystical traditions across cultures

4. End‑Times Myths as Structural Warnings

Myths such as:

• Armageddon (Christianity)
• Ragnarök (Norse mythology)
• Kali Yuga (Hinduism)
• The Five Suns (Aztec cosmology)

all describe the same phenomenon:

Dualistic civilizations inevitably collapse under the weight of their own contradictions.

These myths are not predictions—they are diagnostics.
They describe the structural failure mode of societies that embrace dualism:

• Endless conflict
• Moral absolutism
• Tribalism
• Resource competition
• Breakdown of social cohesion

In this sense, end‑times myths are warnings, not prophecies.

5. Why Dualistic Civilizations Misread Religion

If religious texts encode non‑dualistic wisdom, why did so many civilizations misinterpret them?

The answer is structural:

5.1 Dualistic cultures cannot interpret non‑dualistic texts

A dualistic worldview forces binary interpretations:

• “God vs. Satan”
• “Believer vs. unbeliever”
• “Saved vs. damned”

This makes the original non‑dual message unreadable.

5.2 Power structures depend on dualism

Non‑dualism undermines:

• Hierarchy
• Authority
• Clerical power
• Political control
• Economic privilege

Thus, even if leaders understood the original meaning,
they had incentives to suppress it.

5.3 The result: religion becomes a tool for conflict

Not because religion is flawed,
but because dualistic interpretation corrupts it.

6. Conclusion

Under this framework, religion is not the cause of human conflict.
Rather, religion was originally a harmonizing OS designed to prevent conflict by teaching:

• Non‑dualism
• Coexistence
• Ecological balance
• Social harmony

The true “original sin” is not disobedience—it is dualistic perception.

Once dualism takes hold, civilizations follow a predictable trajectory toward collapse, as encoded in myths across the world.

This interpretation unifies religious texts, mythological structures, and civilizational dynamics into a single coherent model.
